# New Zealand public-sector AI guidance is mostly non-binding, principles-based, and GCDO-led

## Executive answer

- As at 2 July 2026, the clearest whole-of-public-service AI guidance is the Government Chief Digital Officer's Public Service AI Framework, Responsible AI Guidance for the Public Service: GenAI, Public Service AI Work Programme, and Public Service AI Toolkit on Digital.govt.nz. The Framework says it applies to all forms of AI used in New Zealand public services and that agencies are encouraged to align with it, but it is not binding. [Digital.govt.nz, Public Service AI Framework](https://www.digital.govt.nz/standards-and-guidance/technology-and-architecture/artificial-intelligence/public-service-artificial-intelligence-framework)
- The GCDO's current GenAI guidance, updated in February 2025, expands the July 2023 interim GenAI guidance and tells agencies to manage risk through the AI lifecycle, be transparent, train staff, keep humans involved, and use existing privacy, security, procurement, accessibility and information-management obligations. [Digital.govt.nz, Responsible AI Guidance: overview](https://www.digital.govt.nz/standards-and-guidance/technology-and-architecture/artificial-intelligence/responsible-ai-guidance-for-the-public-service-genai/overview)
- Stats NZ's Algorithm Charter for Aotearoa New Zealand and Algorithm Impact Assessment process remain the main official algorithm-governance tools for government agencies using algorithms that could create high or critical unintended harms. The Charter has named government-agency signatories and commits them to transparency, partnership, people, data quality, privacy/ethics/human rights, and human oversight. [Data.govt.nz, Algorithm Charter](https://data.govt.nz/toolkit/data-ethics/government-algorithm-transparency-and-accountability/algorithm-charter)
- New Zealand's national AI policy posture is light-touch, risk-based and built on existing law rather than a standalone AI Act. The Public Service AI Framework and MBIE's AI Strategy material both say existing legal frameworks and existing mechanisms are preferred unless further intervention is needed to unlock innovation or address acute risks. [Digital.govt.nz, Public Service AI Framework](https://www.digital.govt.nz/standards-and-guidance/technology-and-architecture/artificial-intelligence/public-service-artificial-intelligence-framework); [MBIE, Addressing barriers to AI uptake](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/business-and-employment/economic-growth/digital-policy/new-zealands-ai-strategy-investing-with-confidence/addressing-barriers-to-ai-uptake-in-new-zealand)
- I did not find a single mandatory cross-government AI assurance standard in force. The GCDO says a Public Service AI Assurance Regime and extra toolkit resources are under development, and the 2025-2027 work programme includes a future AI assurance model and toolkit. [Digital.govt.nz, next steps for responsible AI](https://www.digital.govt.nz/standards-and-guidance/technology-and-architecture/artificial-intelligence/responsible-ai-guidance-for-the-public-service-genai/next-steps-for-safe-responsible-ai-in-government); [Digital.govt.nz, Public Service AI Work Programme](https://www.digital.govt.nz/standards-and-guidance/technology-and-architecture/artificial-intelligence/public-service-ai-work-programme)

**Overall confidence:** High - the core documents are current official sources and agree on the policy direction. Confidence is lower on agency-level completeness because I did not run an OIA-based survey of every public-sector agency.

## Evidence

### Current public-service AI guidance stack

The GCDO's Public Service AI Framework is the top-level public-service framework I found. It says it supports responsible AI across the New Zealand Public Service, sits within the National AI Strategy launched in July 2025, sets a vision, defines five principles, outlines the policy context, and outlines six pillars of the GCDO AI work programme. It also says agencies are encouraged to align with the Framework, but that the Framework is not binding. [Digital.govt.nz, Public Service AI Framework](https://www.digital.govt.nz/standards-and-guidance/technology-and-architecture/artificial-intelligence/public-service-artificial-intelligence-framework)

The Framework's five principles are based on the OECD values-based AI principles: inclusive and sustainable development; human-centred values; transparency and explainability; safety and security; and accountability. It says public-service AI should respect the rule of law, democratic values, human rights and labour rights; publicly disclose relevant AI use; treat security as a core business requirement; and remain subject to human accountability and oversight. [Digital.govt.nz, Public Service AI Framework](https://www.digital.govt.nz/standards-and-guidance/technology-and-architecture/artificial-intelligence/public-service-artificial-intelligence-framework)

The Framework's policy-context section lists existing laws and public-sector obligations that matter for AI use, including Te Tiriti o Waitangi, the Official Information Act 1982, the New Zealand Bill of Rights Act 1990, the Human Rights Act 1993, the Copyright Act 1994, the Public Records Act 2005, the Public Service Act 2020, and the Privacy Act 2020. [Digital.govt.nz, Public Service AI Framework](https://www.digital.govt.nz/standards-and-guidance/technology-and-architecture/artificial-intelligence/public-service-artificial-intelligence-framework)

The GCDO says it released updated GenAI guidance in February 2025, expanding the July 2023 interim guidance after agency feedback. The same GCDO page says the GCDO has surveyed public-service AI use since 2023 and that, in 2025, 70 government organisations reported 272 AI use cases. [Digital.govt.nz, GCDO's role in AI](https://www.digital.govt.nz/standards-and-guidance/technology-and-architecture/artificial-intelligence/gcdo-role)

### What the GenAI guidance recommends

The Responsible AI Guidance for the Public Service: GenAI is aimed at leaders, decision-makers and people in the New Zealand Public Service who are working with, or planning to work with, GenAI. It says the guidance helps agencies explore and adopt GenAI safely, transparently and responsibly, and should be read with the Public Service AI Framework and the OECD AI system lifecycle. [Digital.govt.nz, Responsible AI Guidance: overview](https://www.digital.govt.nz/standards-and-guidance/technology-and-architecture/artificial-intelligence/responsible-ai-guidance-for-the-public-service-genai/overview)

The overview says agencies should actively assess and manage GenAI risks early and throughout the system life, be open and transparent about GenAI use, right-size risk management, and provide training so staff can use and manage GenAI safely. It also recommends stakeholder involvement at every lifecycle stage, good-quality data, validation of outputs, transparent deployment, and ongoing monitoring for intended and unintended effects. [Digital.govt.nz, Responsible AI Guidance: overview](https://www.digital.govt.nz/standards-and-guidance/technology-and-architecture/artificial-intelligence/responsible-ai-guidance-for-the-public-service-genai/overview)

The GenAI governance guidance recommends that agencies publicly develop and share GenAI policies and standards, designate a responsible senior official to guide safe and secure GenAI adoption, keep accountable humans involved in GenAI use and outputs, and publish information about AI use unless there is a reasonable exception such as a classified use case. [Digital.govt.nz, Governance and GenAI](https://www.digital.govt.nz/standards-and-guidance/technology-and-architecture/artificial-intelligence/responsible-ai-guidance-for-the-public-service-genai/genai-foundations/governance)

The transparency guidance says agencies need to be open with the public about when, why and how they use AI, create policies that say when GenAI can and cannot be used, maintain processes for explaining AI use, and publish an up-to-date GenAI-use register. [Digital.govt.nz, Transparency and GenAI](https://www.digital.govt.nz/standards-and-guidance/technology-and-architecture/artificial-intelligence/responsible-ai-guidance-for-the-public-service-genai/customer-experience/transparency)

The accountability guidance says humans must be involved when using GenAI, that GenAI should not replace judgement or decision-making, and that higher-risk or higher-impact use should follow the agency's risk and assurance framework and controls. It also points to an AI impact assessment for significant work. [Digital.govt.nz, Accountability and GenAI](https://www.digital.govt.nz/standards-and-guidance/technology-and-architecture/artificial-intelligence/responsible-ai-guidance-for-the-public-service-genai/genai-foundations/accountability-responsibility)

The privacy guidance says the Privacy Act 2020 applies to GenAI as it does to other technology, recommends privacy impact assessments for testing or using GenAI, and says information entered into a public GenAI system must already be public or acceptable to make public. [Digital.govt.nz, Privacy and GenAI](https://www.digital.govt.nz/standards-and-guidance/technology-and-architecture/artificial-intelligence/responsible-ai-guidance-for-the-public-service-genai/customer-experience/privacy)

The security guidance says agencies should treat GenAI like other software applications, complete security risk assessment based on factors such as data access, users, development path and external data sharing, evaluate vendor GenAI changes before rollout, and prevent unapproved use where needed because public systems can create data-breach risks. [Digital.govt.nz, Security and GenAI](https://www.digital.govt.nz/standards-and-guidance/technology-and-architecture/artificial-intelligence/responsible-ai-guidance-for-the-public-service-genai/genai-foundations/security)

The procurement guidance says agencies should first test whether AI is the right solution, plan appropriately, develop robust evaluation criteria, understand suppliers and offerings, and keep the Government Procurement Rules and Government Procurement Principles in view when planning AI procurements. [Digital.govt.nz, Procurement and GenAI](https://www.digital.govt.nz/standards-and-guidance/technology-and-architecture/artificial-intelligence/responsible-ai-guidance-for-the-public-service-genai/genai-foundations/procurement)

The customer-experience guidance says agencies need to consider transparency, bias/discrimination/fairness/equity, accessibility, privacy, and Māori, Pacific Peoples and ethnic communities when GenAI affects service experience. Its fairness section says GenAI can disproportionately affect Māori, Pacific Peoples, disabled people, LGBTIQ+ communities and multicultural communities, and recommends partner/community involvement, diverse teams and critical validation of outputs. [Digital.govt.nz, GenAI and customer experience](https://www.digital.govt.nz/standards-and-guidance/technology-and-architecture/artificial-intelligence/responsible-ai-guidance-for-the-public-service-genai/customer-experience); [Digital.govt.nz, Bias, discrimination, fairness and equity](https://www.digital.govt.nz/standards-and-guidance/technology-and-architecture/artificial-intelligence/responsible-ai-guidance-for-the-public-service-genai/customer-experience/bias-and-discrimination)

The Māori, Pacific Peoples and ethnic communities guidance says GenAI in public services creates both opportunities and challenges when Māori data is involved or Māori communities are affected, and recommends considering purpose, potential impacts on Māori, the nature and status of Māori data, Māori data-governance applications, and existing Māori-Crown relationship approaches. [Digital.govt.nz, Māori, Pacific Peoples, ethnic communities and GenAI](https://www.digital.govt.nz/standards-and-guidance/technology-and-architecture/artificial-intelligence/responsible-ai-guidance-for-the-public-service-genai/customer-experience/maori-pacific-and-ethnic-communities)

### Work programme and toolkit

The Public Service AI Work Programme lays out a two-year plan to improve public services and modernise government through AI. It says the programme has 15 initiatives and deliverables across four focus areas: common-use tools, safe and responsible AI, customer and partnerships, and AI workforce. [Digital.govt.nz, Public Service AI Work Programme](https://www.digital.govt.nz/standards-and-guidance/technology-and-architecture/artificial-intelligence/public-service-ai-work-programme)

The work programme includes planned or active deliverables such as a Public Service AI Hub with registries, libraries, standards, patterns and tools; an AI Innovation and Accelerator Lab; an AI Sandbox; annual AI use-case measurement; and a Public Service AI assurance model and toolkit. [Digital.govt.nz, Public Service AI Work Programme](https://www.digital.govt.nz/standards-and-guidance/technology-and-architecture/artificial-intelligence/public-service-ai-work-programme)

The Public Service AI Toolkit consolidates resources to help public-sector organisations plan, implement and manage AI safely and responsibly. Its about page says the toolkit currently contains a Use of Artificial Intelligence Policy template and records-management guidance, and that it is not mandatory even though using it helps align with good practice. [Digital.govt.nz, Public Service AI Toolkit](https://www.digital.govt.nz/standards-and-guidance/technology-and-architecture/artificial-intelligence/public-service-ai-toolkit/about-the-ai-toolkit)

The AI policy template is a starting point for agencies and says it sets out the approach, principles and responsibilities for an agency to use AI safely and responsibly in the workplace. It is intended to be read alongside the Public Service AI Framework and Responsible AI Guidance for the Public Service. [Digital.govt.nz, AI policy template for agencies](https://www.digital.govt.nz/standards-and-guidance/technology-and-architecture/artificial-intelligence/public-service-ai-toolkit/use-of-artificial-intelligence-policy-template)

The records-management guidance says AI does not change what counts as a public record, and that records must still be managed under the Public Records Act 2005 regardless of the technology used to create or interact with them. It gives examples for AI meeting summaries, public chatbots, pilots and GenAI-drafted documents. [Digital.govt.nz, Records management for AI](https://www.digital.govt.nz/standards-and-guidance/technology-and-architecture/artificial-intelligence/public-service-ai-toolkit/records-managements-for-ai)

### Stats NZ algorithm governance still matters

The Algorithm Charter for Aotearoa New Zealand was released in July 2020 to increase public confidence and visibility around algorithm use in the public sector. Its page lists 29 current signatories, including Stats NZ, the Department of Internal Affairs, the Public Service Commission, Inland Revenue, the Ministry of Social Development, Oranga Tamariki, Police and several other government agencies. [Data.govt.nz, Algorithm Charter](https://data.govt.nz/toolkit/data-ethics/government-algorithm-transparency-and-accountability/algorithm-charter)

The Charter takes a risk-based approach and focuses on government algorithms that have high or critical risk of unintended harm for New Zealanders. It expressly says the Charter cannot fully address complex data-ethics issues such as Māori data sovereignty. [Data.govt.nz, Algorithm Charter](https://data.govt.nz/toolkit/data-ethics/government-algorithm-transparency-and-accountability/algorithm-charter)

The Charter commitments are to assess the impact of algorithm-informed decisions and apply commitments according to the identified risk rating. The commitments cover transparency, partnership, people, data, privacy/ethics/human rights, and human oversight, including plain-English documentation, explaining algorithm-informed decisions, identifying and managing bias, peer review for unintended consequences, a public-inquiry contact point, and appeal or challenge channels. [Data.govt.nz, Algorithm Charter](https://data.govt.nz/toolkit/data-ethics/government-algorithm-transparency-and-accountability/algorithm-charter)

Stats NZ's Algorithm Impact Assessment User Guide says the AIA process was created to help agencies meet Charter commitments and support informed decisions about benefits and risks of government algorithm use. The process has four components: a threshold assessment, an AIA questionnaire, a user guide and an AIA report template. [Data.govt.nz, Algorithm Impact Assessment user guide](https://data.govt.nz/docs/algorithm-impact-assessment-user-guide)

The AIA guide describes the AIA documentation as a best-practice approach rather than a one-size-fits-all rule, and says agencies can adopt all or parts of the documents depending on their context, risk profile and existing assessments. [Data.govt.nz, Algorithm Impact Assessment user guide](https://data.govt.nz/docs/algorithm-impact-assessment-user-guide)

### Privacy and agency-level policies

The Office of the Privacy Commissioner says the Privacy Act applies to everyone using AI tools in New Zealand and that the Information Privacy Principles set legal requirements for collecting, using and sharing personal information. The Commissioner says a privacy impact assessment before starting, then regular updates, is the best way to understand enough about an AI tool to uphold the IPPs. [Office of the Privacy Commissioner, AI and the IPPs](https://www.privacy.org.nz/resources-and-learning/a-z-topics/ai/)

The Public Service Commission has its own internal AI policy and guidance released through OIA. That policy says Commission AI use must be through approved tools, under meaningful human oversight, with all final decisions made by people, and that public GenAI tools must not receive official, classified, personal or otherwise sensitive information not intended for public release. This is evidence of one agency's policy implementation, not proof of a public-sector-wide rule. [Public Service Commission, OIA release OIA-2025-0123](https://www.publicservice.govt.nz/assets/DirectoryFile/OIA-2025-0123-Information-request-regarding-the-governments-use-of-artificial-intelligence-AI.pdf)

### National policy context

MBIE released New Zealand's first national AI strategy in July 2025 alongside voluntary responsible-AI guidance for businesses. The public announcement says the strategy follows OECD AI principles, including rule of law, human rights, democratic values, fairness, privacy, robustness, security and safety. [MBIE, AI strategy announcement](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/about/news/artificial-intelligence-strategy-and-business-guidance-now-available)

MBIE's strategy material says New Zealand is taking a light-touch and principles-based approach to AI policy, relying on existing technology-neutral frameworks such as privacy, consumer protection and human rights, and updating those frameworks when needed. [MBIE, Addressing barriers to AI uptake](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/business-and-employment/economic-growth/digital-policy/new-zealands-ai-strategy-investing-with-confidence/addressing-barriers-to-ai-uptake-in-new-zealand)

The Public Service AI Framework says Cabinet has agreed to a light-touch, proportionate and risk-based approach to AI regulation, with further intervention considered only to unlock innovation or address acute risks, and with existing mechanisms preferred over a standalone AI Act. [Digital.govt.nz, Public Service AI Framework](https://www.digital.govt.nz/standards-and-guidance/technology-and-architecture/artificial-intelligence/public-service-artificial-intelligence-framework)

## What would change this conclusion

- A Cabinet decision, GCDO standard, Public Service Commissioner standard, procurement rule, or statute making specific AI assurance practices mandatory would change the conclusion that the current system is mostly non-binding guidance.
- Publication of the planned Public Service AI Assurance Regime, the AI assurance model/toolkit, a central AI-use registry, or agency AI registers could materially change the practical answer about what agencies must document and disclose.
- A full agency-by-agency survey or OIA exercise could show that many agencies have more specific internal AI policies than the public guidance reveals. I only checked one PSC OIA release as an example of an agency-level policy.
- This desk review did not verify how agencies actually apply the guidance, whether published use registers exist across agencies, or whether affected communities experience the transparency and challenge pathways described in the guidance.
- This finding uses public official sources only and does not include legal advice. A lawyer should review any claim about enforceability before the project uses this as operational compliance guidance.

## Open follow-up questions

- Which public-service agencies have published AI-use registers, AI policies or GenAI acceptable-use rules, and how complete are they?
- How should the forthcoming Public Service AI Assurance Regime map against the Algorithm Impact Assessment, privacy impact assessments, procurement assurance and public-recordkeeping?
- Which of the 272 AI use cases reported in the 2025 cross-agency survey are public-facing, high-impact, or decision-support uses that should receive stronger transparency treatment?
- How are Māori data governance and Te Tiriti considerations being operationalised in real public-service AI projects?
- What parts of the current guidance apply cleanly to Crown entities, local government, schools, health organisations and council-controlled organisations, and where are the boundary gaps?
